Do riders mix across lines? Income yes, race less

  • Measured segregation of station-to-station flows (Moro segregation index on census block groups), then stress-tested with permutation tests under 8 controls.
  • Income homophily (SI 0.758) is geography: it loses significance once population density is controlled (p 0.41 → 1.0). People ride where people live.
  • Race homophily (SI 0.440) is robust: it survives every control — education, age, income, density (p = 0.0) — except race itself.
  • Translation for policy: income mixing needs no fix; race-line mixing is the real gap infrastructure could address.

The "segregation decline" is expansion, not behavior

Hold the station network fixed (243-station balanced panel) and income SI stays flat ≈ 0.78 while the full system drifts down to ≈ 0.74 — new stations, not changed behavior, drive the decline. Same pattern for race.

Bikes complement the T — they don't replace it

  • On major MBTA disruption days, bike ridership drops slightly: −2.5% naive, −2.2% after weather controls.
  • No substitution effect in either direction — riders treat bike + T as one network.
  • Weather is what actually moves demand: temperature r = +0.66; adding weather lifts model R² from 0.80 to 0.90.

Bike-lane gaps from revealed demand

of routed Boston bike volume rides on streets with no lane on the street itself (within 30 m)
5.3%

  • Where the true gaps concentrate: Roxbury core, South Boston / Broadway, City Point, Mission Hill, and East Boston (395K trips, top-300 flows, OSRM street-routed).
  • Streets one block from an existing facility (e.g. Tremont St beside the Southwest Corridor) are muted as parallel, not counted as gaps.
  • A lower bound — the router already prefers laned streets; Cambridge's network is merged so river corridors score on real infrastructure.

Every trip is a vote for a corridor (11_lane_gap_corridors.csv). Ready-made prioritization input for BTD's protected-lane program.

Next steps with the City

  • Equity-weighted expansion: the 25 MCLP sites (+106,930 residents covered) are a starting shortlist — co-rank with BTD priorities to close the 49.3% vs 23.2% access gap deliberately.
  • Corridor program input: ranked lane-gap corridors delivered — 5.3% of routed volume is truly unprotected (>130 m from any lane); the Roxbury core and South Boston/Broadway lead the list.
  • Annual mixing report: track income/race SI on the balanced panel as a standing equity metric.
  • E-bike siting pilot: concentrate e-bike capacity in low-income tracts and measure the connection effect against the Dec-2023 baseline.
